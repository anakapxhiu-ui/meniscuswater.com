## What's actually in it — the specifics

### The big problem: disinfection byproducts (DBPs)

Austin uses chloramine disinfection (chlorine + ammonia), which reacts with organic matter from the Colorado River to form a cocktail of byproducts. Your specific readings:

| Contaminant | Reading | vs. EWG Guideline |
|---|---|---|
| Bromochloroacetic acid | 4.18 ppb | 209× |
| Bromodichloromethane | 10.1 ppb | 168× |
| Chloroform | 8.67 ppb | 22× |
| Bromoform | 2.60 ppb | 5.2× |

None of these have a federal legal limit individually. EPA regulates them as a combined group ("TTHMs") at 80 ppb, and Austin is under that ceiling — but the combined load here is well above what modern toxicology considers safe for long-term exposure, with associations to bladder cancer, kidney, liver, and central nervous system problems.

### Hexavalent chromium (chromium-6)

0.175 ppb — 8.7× EWG's health guideline of 0.02 ppb. This is the "Erin Brockovich" contaminant — a known carcinogen with no federal limit, though California set a public health goal of 0.02 ppb.

### PFAS — genuinely good news

Austin Water tested quarterly for 29 PFAS compounds under EPA's Fifth Unregulated Contaminant Monitoring Rule from October 2023 through 2024. Only six PFAS were intermittently detected — all below the EPA's new 4 ppt maximum contaminant level. The Highland Lakes have had a ban on wastewater pollutant discharges since the late 1980s, and water from the Handcox Water Treatment Plant showed no detectable PFAS at all. This is rare — the EPA estimates 6–10% of public water systems will fail the new PFAS guidelines, and about 50 Texas water systems already exceed the new limits.

### Lead — essentially a non-issue structurally

In October 2024, Austin Water completed a multi-year inventory and confirmed its public water system contains no lead pipes. Your home was built 2001–2005, post-dating the 1986 lead solder ban, so internal plumbing lead risk is also low.

### Chloramine itself

Austin uses it as the primary disinfectant. Most basic carbon filters don't handle chloramine well — you need a filter specifically certified for chloramine reduction (catalytic carbon, not just activated carbon).
